The Canterville Ghost
Archive links

Original story by Oscar Wilde
Adapted for the stage by Darwin Reid Payne

Based on Oscar Wilde’s delightful story written in 1887, the tale of The Otis', a turn-of-the-century American family moving into an English manor haunted by an old ghost, is still fresh and funny today.

Oscar Wilde, in his usual style, makes fun of everyone, including the "stuck in the past" British and the hardy Americans who still love everything British, including their ghosts.

Although Mrs. Umney, the hilarious British housekeeper, and the Cantervilles, the former residents, are deathly afraid of Sir Simon, the American family is delighted to have a real English ghost for their very own. Rather than being afraid, the family members try to befriend Sir Simon. The old ghost is humiliated when he uses all his tricks to scare them, but to no avail. When he gets out his clanking chains, Mr. Otis offers him some "Rising Sun Marvel Lubricating Oil," to help him keep down the noise. The two young daughters clean up the "scary" blood stain with "Pinkerton's Champion Stain Remover," causing him to have to use a paint set with only green paint to paint it back on. Then the little girls offer to help him haunt the house as three stories is too much for one small ghost. He tries to explain that it is proper English behavior to be afraid of ghosts, but the family continues to be undisturbed. Finally, the older daughter, Virginia, manages to soothe Sir Simon's hurt feelings and finds out that he is lonely and would like nothing better than to be at rest. With Virginia's kindness and bravery, he finds a way to end his haunts and leave Canterville Manor for good.
